Hearing: An introduction

Why want it? Why do you want to hear things? Why is it important ?


Hearing is undoubtedly one of the most important senses that we possess. From an evolutionary standpoint, it allows us to be better aware of our surroundings, such as recognizing and reacting to sounds of threats or warnings. Such as something like a snake’s rattle or a predator trying to sneak up on you. Today, this would be better exemplified by a siren, horn, or emergency alert. The ability to use our sense of hearing in combination with our brain’s ability to process and comprehend different sounds as languages also play a major role in our survival and overall quality of life. Although ‘language’ is a vastly complex concept that humans and animals can both utilize effectively without the use of hearing, It would be counterproductive not to recognize the role that hearing plays in our way of communication. This communication then enables us to learn, instruct, and engage in meaningful discussions. This is important, considering that we are a very social species. Being able to effectively communicate and socialize again greatly improves our mental health and overall quality of life. This is why it’s very important to provide ways to allow people who have difficulty hearing or are deaf to be able to access other ways of effective communication, such as sign language or subtitles.How does it work!

How does it all work?

Fundamentally, like most other major senses, hearing involves having outside environmental stimuli converted by our sensory organs (in this case, the ears) into electrical signals that are interpreted by a certain part of the brain. Hearing starts with sound waves. I will get into more detail soon. These soundwaves are first funneled into the outer ear. The outer ear consists of the ear itself and the ear canal. The sound waves make their way to the middle ear. The middle ear has three small bones. As you probably already know are some of the smallest bones in the Human body. They include the hammer, anvil, and stirrup. The middle ear also includes the ear drum. When sound waves hit the ear drum, it causes the ear drum to vibrate, and the waves are then transmitted through the three little bones to the inner ear. The inner ear is where the sound waves are processed and converted to signals for the brain to interpret. The cochlea is a snail-shaped structure or one of those shells from the beach, that is filled with fluid and hair cells. The fluid moves the fluid, which moves the hairs that have a lot of nerve endings attached to them. Those nerve endings then send the signals. Of course, it’s the brain’s job to then interpret those signals, and not everyone will interpret a certain sound or frequency the same. Different music tastes, perhaps.Sound Waves!

What are sound waves? What makes sound? What is that noise? What is that melody??

Sound is produced when an object vibrates. This creates a ‘pressure wave’. A wave is essentially just energy flowing through a medium. In this case, the medium would be the air hence why you wouldn’t be able to hear in space. However, sound can travel through any medium, like water. Just not as easily. In a lot of ways, sound waves are like light waves. They both originate from a definite source and can be refracted and dampened in a number of ways.

There are also 4 basic elements of a sound wave.

  • • Frequency (pitch) o Think of Higher or lower sounds
  • • Amplitude (dynamics) o Think , loudness. How loud or quiet is it.
  • • Timbre (Tone Color) o Think of different types of instruments. The tone is different on the piano than on the lute.
  • • Duration (tempo) o Think , how long does this note last or is sustained?

The science of waves themselves is a very interesting and complex topic, but we’re going to talk a little about what kind of sound waves our ears are able to pick up. What Can We Hear!

What are we hearing right now?

The frequency of a sound wave is measured in Hz. The Human ear can pick up frequencies from about 20 Hz to about 20 kHz, or 20,000 Hz. Interestingly, most people become less sensitive to high frequencies over time, so the average limit for adults is more so 15 kHz to 17 kHz. The sounds that we cannot hear have names for those sound waves. Anything below 20 Hz is considered an infrasonic wave. Anything above 20 kHz is considered an Ultrasonic wave. So even though we can’t actually hear these frequencies, they are still very useful to us. Geologists often use infrasonic waves to detect earthquakes, and of course, in medicine, the Ultrasound uses Ultrasonic waves. Soothing frequencies

What frequencies do we find most pleasant? White, pink, or brown noise?

White noise is technically a noise that contains all frequencies across the entire sound spectrum that we can observe. It kind of sounds more like the ocean, wind, or tv static. I found that the difference between the three sounds mostly had to do with the amplitude of different ends of the spectrum. For example, pink noise has louder low-end frequencies and softer hi-end. Brown is pretty much just an exaggerated pink with an even lower low-end that sounds more like a rumble. Kind of like the sound you hear when you’re in an airplane. People often use fans and other things similar to that to produce sustained white noise. Research has shown that exposure to this sustained mashup of frequencies can be beneficial to our well-being in a few different ways, like improving sleep, reducing crying babies, and improving focus.Summary
